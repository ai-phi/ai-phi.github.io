Pierre Bessiere will discuss four key points made in his latest book (released nov. 20, [here](https://laboutique.edpsciences.fr/produit/1494/9782759838301/intelligences)):

1. The first message is that **for an intelligence, having a ‚Äúbody‚Äù changes everything**. Natural intelligences, from Chlamy, a single-celled algae, to humans, all have bodies. The most powerful artificial intelligences do not. Robots have bodies but are still very rudimentary.
2. For artificial intelligences, in the absence of a body, ‚Äúmeaning‚Äù is only given by a human user. Without this human mediator, **artificial intelligences are meaningless**. They perform calculations, but these have no impact on their environment unless a human intervenes.
3. For natural intelligences, having a body allows them to interact directly with their environment. This interaction, and the resulting actions and perceptions, is what gives ‚Äúmeaning‚Äù and allows us to predict what is likely to happen: if I perceive this, then I will do that and I will perceive that. 4. The Truth is inaccessible to them, but Reason, the art of making the best use of the little information available to them, is indispensable. The book therefore compares two models of reasoning: logic and probability. Logic, the current foundation of our computers, which deals only with Truth. Probability, which allows us to reason while being ignorant, with incomplete and uncertain information. **Probability is indispensable for reasoning correctly while being ignorant.**
