## What is a Causerie?

A traditional seminar typically showcases a single expert's perspective,
often leaving the audience in the background, in a passive role. In
contrast, we wanted to create a space where individuals could actively
contribute, bringing their unique research contexts and personalities
into an open-form, relaxed discussion. To achieve this, we took
inspiration from the French concept of a _causerie_---a term
originating from the Parisian salons, where intellectuals engaged in
informal yet thought-provoking discussion.

## LLMs for French Secondary Education: Ethical Questions and Applied Research

_Summary of an AI-Phi Causerie_

This document presents a structured summary of a session held within the **AI-Phi (AI & Philosophy) Causerie series**, an informal discussion format inspired by early modern intellectual salons, designed to foster open, exploratory, and interdisciplinary exchanges. Unlike formal seminars, causeries prioritise dialogue, critical engagement, and the collective refinement of ideas.

The session featured a presentation by Thomas Souverain on the development and evaluation of large language models (LLMs) for French secondary education, with a particular focus on **alignment, robustness, watermarking, and regulatory interpretation**. The discussion situated these technical challenges within broader ethical, pedagogical, and institutional contexts.

---

## 1. Project Context: OpenLLM France and Educational AI

The presentation was framed within the **OpenLLM France consortium (2024–2026)**, a collaborative initiative aiming to develop a **sovereign, transparent, and EU-compliant large language model** adapted to educational use. The project integrates multiple research directions, including:

- model alignment with educational norms,
- evaluation methodologies,
- safety and robustness,
- and compliance with emerging European regulatory frameworks.

Education is not treated merely as an application domain, but as a **highly constrained and normatively charged environment**, where the deployment of LLMs raises immediate questions regarding trust, accountability, and appropriate use.

---

## 2. Educational Use Cases and Emerging Tensions

Current and anticipated uses of LLMs in secondary education include:

- summarisation and revision support,
- automated feedback and quiz generation,
- writing assistance and language practice,
- and personalised tutoring.

While these applications offer clear pedagogical benefits, they also generate structural tensions. In particular, the discussion highlighted the risk that effective tools may incentivise strategic misuse. This dynamic was characterised as a **“negative phase of gamification”**, whereby increased system utility correlates with increased attempts to circumvent its intended use.

This observation underscores a broader point: **educational AI systems must be evaluated not only in terms of capability, but also in terms of the behaviours they induce.**

---

## 3. Multi-Layered Evaluation and Validation

A central methodological contribution of the session was the articulation of a **multi-layered evaluation framework** for educational LLMs, extending beyond standard performance benchmarks. This framework includes:

1. **Alignment evaluation**  
   Involving educators and domain experts to assess the appropriateness and pedagogical relevance of outputs.

2. **Robustness testing**  
   Including adversarial evaluation (e.g. prompt injection, misuse scenarios, data poisoning).

3. **User-level validation**  
   Assessing usability, acceptability, and integration into teaching practices.

4. **Institutional validation (conditional)**  
   Potential endorsement or integration into official educational infrastructures.

This layered approach reflects the necessity of integrating **technical, social, and institutional criteria** in the evaluation of AI systems deployed in education.

---

## 4. Watermarking and the Detection of AI-Generated Text

A substantial portion of the session was devoted to **watermarking techniques**, understood as methods for identifying whether a given piece of text has been generated by an AI system.

In the educational context, watermarking serves as a potential mechanism for:

- distinguishing between student-authored and AI-generated work,
- supporting academic integrity,
- and enabling traceability of synthetic content.

However, the discussion emphasised that watermarking is not merely a technical tool, but also an **ethical intervention**, aimed at preserving meaningful distinctions between different forms of authorship.

---

## 5. Technical Constraints: The Specificity of Text

A key technical challenge arises from the nature of text as a medium. Unlike images or video, where imperceptible noise can be embedded robustly, text is:

- highly sensitive to small perturbations,
- semantically structured,
- and directly interpretable by human readers.

As a result, watermarking text involves delicate trade-offs between:

- invisibility,
- robustness to attacks,
- and preservation of linguistic quality.

---

## 6. Threat Model: Extractability and Erasability

The session introduced a particularly useful conceptual distinction between two primary threats to watermarking systems:

- **Extractability (or forgery):**  
  The risk that a watermark can be copied or injected into unrelated or malicious content.

- **Erasability:**  
  The risk that users can remove or degrade the watermark through minimal modifications.

The latter was illustrated through the notion of the **“smart cheater”**: an agent who uses AI-generated content as a base, then performs light edits to evade detection while retaining most of the generated structure.

This dual threat model highlights the fundamental requirement that watermarking systems be both:

- resistant to imitation, and
- resistant to removal.

---

## 7. The Landscape of Watermarking Approaches

Watermarking methods can be implemented at multiple stages of the LLM pipeline, including:

- modifications to training data distributions,
- embedding signals within model architecture or weights,
- biasing token selection during generation,
- or applying transformations at the post-processing stage.

Each approach involves trade-offs in terms of:

- detectability,
- robustness,
- computational cost,
- and impact on model performance.

The discussion converged on the view that **no single method is sufficient**, and that effective watermarking will likely require a **multi-layered combination of techniques**.

---

## 8. Regulatory Interpretation: The EU AI Act

The session also examined the implications of the **European AI Act**, which requires that AI-generated content be marked in a machine-readable manner using techniques that are:

- reliable,
- robust,
- effective,
- and interoperable.

A key point raised was that these terms remain **insufficiently defined** at the technical level. The presentation proposed an initial operational clarification:

- **reliability** as detectability of the watermark,
- **effectiveness** as preservation of output quality and usability,
- **robustness** as resistance to adversarial manipulation,
- **interoperability** as compatibility across systems (currently underdeveloped).

This reflects a broader structural gap between **legal language and technical implementation**, requiring active interpretation and standardisation efforts.

---

## 9. Standards, Governance, and Research Dynamics

The discussion highlighted the role of:

- expert working groups,
- standards bodies,
- and codes of practice,

in translating regulatory requirements into actionable guidelines.

It also pointed to the existence of **divergent incentives among stakeholders**, with some actors favouring rapid standardisation, and others benefiting from regulatory delay.

In this context, technical research on watermarking becomes entangled with **questions of governance, coordination, and institutional power**.

---

## 10. Red Teaming and Deployment Realities

Finally, the session addressed ongoing work on **red teaming educational LLMs**, involving:

- systematic adversarial testing,
- simulation of misuse scenarios,
- and identification of system vulnerabilities.

This approach reflects a shift toward **anticipatory evaluation**, where systems are tested not only for expected use cases, but for their behaviour under adversarial conditions.

---

## Conclusion

The development of LLMs for secondary education illustrates the convergence of **technical, ethical, and regulatory challenges**. The session highlighted several key insights:

- Educational contexts amplify the stakes of LLM deployment.
- Evaluation must extend beyond performance to include alignment, robustness, and usability.
- Watermarking is both necessary and intrinsically limited, especially in text-based systems.
- Regulatory requirements require technical interpretation to become operational.
- Effective solutions will likely be **multi-layered, adaptive, and continuously updated**.

More broadly, the discussion suggests that the responsible deployment of educational AI systems depends less on any single technical solution than on the **ongoing coordination between research, policy, and practice**.
